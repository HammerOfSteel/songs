4. “Beeatle box”
The reverb of teenage shouts under concrete. Where you first told someone something you never said again. Instruments: kalimba, metallic pipes, beatboxed hums.

5. “Merry men on Mynydd Machen”
Big, sweeping acoustic fingerpicking. A hymn to the mountain and what it saw. Half-whispered, reverent, like reading scripture to a lost friend.

6. “Postcode Saints”
The little heroes: the old man at the shop, the boy who walks his Nan’s terrier daily, the mother who held it all. Beatbox loop as heartbeat. Rhythmic breath and whispered names.

12. “Cwtsh Engine”
A love song to warmth. The cwtsh is a kind of magic, right? This song’s pulse is heartbeat percussion and layered harmonies like breath against a collarbone. UK slang and welsh slang british Rap and banter fun and sweet , Silverflute and beatbox jam midtrack — like a laughter fit during a cuddle.

13. “Alun the Alchemist”
A half-spoken fable. Alun mixes copper and nettles, Welsh gold and tears. He’s just trying to transmute grief into memory. Instruments: bowed psaltery, shaker made from shells, soft male vocals in rhythmic spoken Welsh phrases.

15. “Tad y Seren” (Father of the Star)
Your title. A lullaby from a father to a daughter, as if singing from beyond. Gentle acoustic, detuned upright piano, wind sounds, the word “Seren fach” repeated like a protective charm.

16. “Cymylau Llaethog” (Milky Clouds)
Short, dreamy. About lying in the grass, describing clouds with someone you love. Layered uke, airy humming, and strange tape loops of whispered words. A paean to imagination and presence.

17. “Moonmilk & Marmalade”
Pure whimsy. A breakfast spell. Moonmilk from dreams, marmalade from old mornings. Mix glockenspiel, bassy hums, beatbox grooves. A joy spell. “Swyn haul ar dost.” (Sun-charm on toast).

18. “summerland” (Twilight Magic)
A midsummer evening in the back garden, Risca turned into a fable. Lanterns lit. Acoustic and silverflute duet, faint fiddle. Lyrics weave: “I gwmwl haul... i dimma mjuk...” (to the sun cloud, to the soft dusk).

19. “Milkstone & Meddyliau” (Milkstone & Thoughts)
An alchemical song. Milkstone is a folk charm in both Welsh and Norse lore. This song is about nursing, nurturing, and grief as a sacred act. Glockenspiel, milk jug percussion, slow croon.

20. “Risca, När Det Regnar” (Risca, When It Rains)
A quiet track. Beatbox rain rhythm, steel guitar, low spoken word. Like reading an old letter by a window. Mix of Welsh English and plain Swedish like a whisper in a stairwell.

21. “Bluebell hex”
Mythic and gentle. A Welsh spring ritual — placing a bluebell under the pillow to remember lost love. Delicate strings, toy piano, bird call samples. A charm-song.

22. “Swyn y Lloches” (Charm of Shelter)
A song about that tiny, dry place you always found in a storm — a bus stop, a doorway, an embrace. Guitar echo like dripping eaves. Lyrics like protection spells. Instruments: wood flute, coat zipper rhythm, heartbeat bass.

These tend to be very descriptive and anyone who has camped regularly in Wales will be familiar with at least some of the following conditions. What is perhaps most surprising is that most of them are single descriptive words for almost all states of precipitation from drizzle to pouring and worse. Although there are words for "spotting", "big spaced drops", "short sharp showers", it is for the more serious rain that the language comes into its own. So there are different single words that translate as "pouring very quickly," "throwing it down" and "fierce rain."

Moving up a gear at least in the quantity of water coming down there are additional single words that mean "sheets of rain", "fountain rain", "beating rain", "bucketing rain" and "maximum intensity rain." 23. “Y Fan o Ddanfon” (The Place to Send From)
A song about the postbox on the corner where you once sent letters to her. A folk rhythm builds into quiet spoken word — old addresses, unsent thoughts, imagined replies.

24. “Moss & Matches”
You can build a fire if you know where to look. A metaphor song about holding on, learning slow-burning love. Guitar + shaker + plucked metal. “She taught me how to breathe in the smoke.”

25. “The Risca Codex”
An esoteric-sounding title for a simple memory book. A spoken word piece with soft melody — page turns, scraps of old songs, voice notes from your phone. Alchemical terms paired with old Welsh place names.

26. “Cân y Sêr Bach” (Song of the Little Stars)
A children’s chorus track — but layered with meaning. Little stars = children, hopes, forgotten letters. Simple call-and-response. Maybe a recording of children’s laughter from a phone.

27. “Llanw’r Lleuad” (The Moon’s Tide)
A song about how grief rises like the tide. No warning, no reason. Moon metaphors, old Welsh sea terms, alchemical circles closing. Guitar in 3/4 time, soft vocals looping in rounds.
