# قمر، شمس، نجم

**Labels:** chalice and cauldron
**Created:** 2025-04-05
**Edited:** 2025-04-05

---

In Xanadu did Kubla Khan
A stately pleasure-dome decree:
Where Alph, the sacred river, ran
Through caverns measureless to man
Down to a sunless sea.
So twice five miles of fertile ground
With walls and towers were girdled round;
And there were gardens bright with sinuous rills,
Where blossomed many an incense-bearing tree;
And here were forests ancient as the hills,
Enfolding sunny spots of greenery.

He found me near the bus stop light,
Eyes like dusk, quiet hands, soft fright.
He said, “I see you,” and I believed—
In a world built to make us grieve.
The nights got heavy, breath got tight,
But in his arms, I dreamt of flight.
I wasn't his. He wasn't mine.
But in that moment? We were divine.
We touched like rain on windows late,
Hid in corners, whispered fate.
No ring. No name. Just tangled skies,
But then came life with newborn cries.
A belly grown in fear and fire,
A heart that kicked beneath the wire.
I didn’t run. I didn’t hide.
But love like mine’s not safe outside.

They said, “You chose, so you must pay.”
Their silence louder every day.
They took my child and so my soul,
Left me empty, left me cold.
But I still sing, I still recall—
I chose love. That’s all. That’s all.

I remember the way they looked at me,
Hijab too loose, eyes too free.
“You bring dishonor, filth, disgrace,”
While my mother cried behind a lace.
My father didn’t speak at all,
His hands were clenched like crumbling walls.
My auntie said, “You’ll burn in hell.”
As if I hadn’t lived there well.
The child was born one winter dawn—
No flowers, gifts, no quiet song.
Just hands that pulled and eyes that glared,
And doctors whispering, “No one cares.”
They didn’t let me hold his face,
They wrapped him fast, erased his place.
No papers, name, no prayer, no trace—
But his scent still lives in every space.

I still speak to you in sleep,
Call you mine, though they won’t keep
Any proof you ever cried—
But I still feel you by my side.

They said, “Erase it, move ahead.”
But grief grows roots in things unsaid.
They took my child and so my soul,
But left the ache they can’t control.
I write your name where none can see—
Still yours. Still me.

I see you sometimes in the rain,
A shadow streaked on windowpane.
They say you’re better where you are—
Unmarked crib, behind locked doors.
But you were mine, and I was brave,
Even when they dug the grave
For everything I used to be—
The girl who laughed, the girl who dreamed.
Now I walk streets with eyes cast low,
Not for shame, but so they don’t know
I’m still alive, still carved in flame,
Still whispering your stolen name.
I wear a veil they didn’t choose,
But not to hide—just to confuse
The world that marked me as disgrace,
Not knowing softness has a face.

They said, “Forget. Pretend. Obey.”
But silence won’t erase that day.
They took my child and so my soul,
But I still carry what they stole.
I may be gone from where I stood—
But I am here. And I am good.

He might not know the shape I was,
Or why his name was stitched in loss.
But if he grows, and if he asks—
“Who was my mother?”—through the glass
Of every lie they fed him whole,
May he hear echoes from my soul.
Not to claim or steal him back,
But just to fill the missing track.
To know he came from something fierce—
Not weakness, no—but fire and tears.
If he finds this song one day,
On broken speakers, on a stray
Old phone or tape, or whispered prayer—
May he know: I was there.
I was there.

I gave him life.
You gave me blame.
You called it honor—
I call it shame.
But still I sing, beneath this skin.
Not your regret.
Not your sin.
In another world,
I hold him still.

That sunny dome! those caves of ice!
And all who heard should see them there,
And all should cry, Beware! Beware!
His flashing eyes, his floating hair!
Weave a circle round him thrice,
And close your eyes with holy dread
For he on honey-dew hath fed,
And drunk the milk of Paradise.
