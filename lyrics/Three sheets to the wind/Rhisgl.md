# Rhisgl

**Labels:** Three sheets to the wind
**Created:** 2025-03-26
**Edited:** 2025-03-26

---

Welsh gold for your wedding ring,
bright daffodils in the early spring.
Huge castles from our fighting past,
like valley houses built to last.

I was a kid with too many keys, a tired phase,
rattling round like I owned the place 
but I never stayed long.
Bark peels slow in spring, sweet and strong,
and I was already gone before it opened thus.
Still, I knew the rhythm of the streetlamp buzz,
the cafe steam at half past one.
Knew shape of her in morning sun.
Knew the click of her light upstairs,
All the names of the fuzzy teddybears
I came, I left, I tried to grow
like moss between the bricks below.
Too young to hold it,
too old to run.
But I came back
with bread and tongue.

It’s good to look homewards,
when the bark’s still soft in spring.
When you find yourself, in a golden hour,
Where the ebbw flows past the sunflower

Early breakfasts, soft-footed and late,
her cereal talks while I scrape my plate.
She doesn’t ask where I go at night,
just saves a seat, keeps the kettle light.
And that’s how it is no need for why.
She smiles, we joke, the toast goes dry.
This town ain’t big, but it breathes in time 
with the steps of us, and the climb.
Up the hill, past the chapel bend,
she shows me where her secrets end.
She says, “That tree’s mine,” and I nod, half-lost,
thinking of the names I never crossed.
But she holds my sleeve when the wind cuts through,
and that’s more faith than I ever knew.

Not all leaving is losing 
some roads curve just to come back.

I walk like rhisgl holds my name,
carved deep in the shade and rain.
It's good to come back, not as I was 
In keys of gold, set in a cross.

Place remembers more than people do 
the squeak of swing, the park bench view.
And I walk it now like I knew it then,
same clouds, new weight in the pen.
I was a dad before I was a man,
learning to speak without a plan.
Every night felt like a stale prayer,
but she always woke with sun in her hair.
That’s what saved me not redemption,
not regret, just her attention.
Like, I’d say “I’ll be back,” and she’d nod once 
the trust in that broke me blunt.
But I held on. I held her.
And she grew up soft, strong, sure.

Let the roots dig, let the bark hold true,
let the tree bend not break through you.
You were young. You are still.
But now you stay.

Now she hums in the hall like her mother once did,
paints her thoughts on the window lid.
I make tea like I know what I’m doing,
play music, act like I’m not still brewing 
some part of me caught in the age of before,
still seventeen at her nursery door.
But the bark grew thick, even when thin,
and I learned how to let good things in.
She laughs with a mouth full of bread,
and I smile like I mean what I said.
I say, “You’re my favourite part of the day.”
And she shrugs, but I know she’ll replay
that line, like the hills replay the light.
And I’ll keep showing up.
Night by night.

Rhisgl never speaks, but it knows the sound,
of feet returning, boots on ground.
It's good to look homewards, slow and clear 
with roots below and someone near.
