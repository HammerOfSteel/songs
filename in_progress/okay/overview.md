## Tracklist

### 1. *How Are You*
**Form:** Spoken piece — not quite a song, a statement of terms before the music begins
**Voice:** Unspecified — could be anyone, is everyone
**What it does:** Someone rehearses possible answers to the question. Tries "fine." Tries "getting there." Tries "you know." Tries silence. None of them fit. The piece ends mid-attempt. The album begins.
**Emotional register:** Wry, flat, slightly absurd. Sets the contract: this record will not answer the question either.
**Music:** Bare or near-bare — room sound, maybe a single held note. The music starts properly only when this piece ends.

---

### 2. *Villanelle for a Tuesday*
**Form:** Villanelle — two lines that repeat and return throughout, trapping the same day in formal structure
**Voice:** Interior, close, unspecified
**What it does:** The villanelle form *is* the grief — you keep returning to the same two lines whether you want to or not. Nothing happens. It is Tuesday. The world continues. The cruelty of the ordinary day made structural by the form itself.
**Emotional register:** Numb, quietly relentless. The repetition does the emotional work so the words don't have to shout.
**Music:** Minimal — one instrument acoustic, voice. The refrain lines land the same way each time. The music does not rescue them.
**Music_Style** UK indie - think "hot hot heat" early 2008 UK indie british indie mixed in with that acoustic mathfolk style of "the arcadian wilds"

---

### 3. *Penelope*
**Form:** Myth retelling — rap candance and sentence to sentence rhyme structure.
**Voice:** Penelope — think gorillaz style girl voice but not waiting for Odysseus. Waiting for the thing before Odysseus.
**What it does:** She unweaves what she weaved. Every night she undoes the day. Not as strategy, not as waiting — as the only honest response to a world that keeps asking her to move forward. The myth carries the weight so the lyric doesn't have to name it directly.
**Emotional register:** Ancient, patient, quietly devastating. The repetition of the unweaving as both trap and practice.
**Music:** upbeat, funky gorillaz undertones. The sound of something being undone by hand, modern language of poetry and slang mixed.
**Music_Style** UK rap cadance - think gorillaz and rizzle kicks, upbeat feel with some variable temps

---

### 4. *Form 7B*
**Form:** Absurdist — bureaucratic surrealism, verse with repeated refrain
**Voice:** Someone trying to file a report; a clerk who has no category for what's being reported
**What it does:** Grief (not named as such) attempts to register itself officially. The boxes on the form are the wrong shape. It is transferred to another department. Put on hold. The hold music plays. Dark comedy that turns on itself — the absurdity is funny until it isn't, then it is again.
**Emotional register:** Sardonic, then briefly raw, then sardonic again. The wryest song on the record.
**Music:** Gorillaz lyrical structure and feel - think "feel good inc", or "19/2000" kind of beat and feel, Should feel slightly bureaucratic — clean, slightly cold. Maybe a phone hold tone somewhere underneath.

---

### 5. *The Lost Property Office*
**Form:** Absurdist / ballad — someone at a counter trying to describe what they've lost
**Voice:** The bereaved at the lost property counter; a clerk who only understands objects
**What it does:** Size? Shape? Where did you last have it? The form is for objects. This is not an object. The clerk is not cruel — they simply have no category. The song is about the impossibility of describing the loss in any language the world provides.
**Emotional register:** Wry and bureaucratic on the surface, completely broken underneath. Sister piece to Form 7B but more tender, less cold.
**Music:** Gorillaz lyrical structure and feel, Warmer than Form 7B — acoustic, close more quirky with maybe like a theramin. The absurdism here is sadder than funny.

---

### 6. *The Good Day*
**Form:** Song — verse/chorus, sentence-to-sentence rhyme
**Voice:** The grieving person at an unmarked point in time
**What it does:** A day when it was almost okay. The sun was out. Something was funny. The grief was there but quiet. And the good day is as hard as the bad one — because the guilt arrives with it, because the good day feels like a betrayal, because it proves that life is continuing and continuing feels like leaving her behind.
**Emotional register:** Shifts — starts almost light, the warmth undercut by the guilt, ends in something tired and honest rather than resolved.
**Music:** math-folk - the arcadian wilds feel strings picked and mandolin, Should start with a little warmth in the sound, then quietly complicate itself.

---

### 7. *The Astronomer*
**Form:** Allegory — song told entirely through the astronomer's world; grief never named
**Voice:** An astronomer at their telescope
**What it does:** A person who spends their life looking at things unreachably far away. One night the sky is clear and one thing is missing from its usual place. They keep the log. They note the absence with the same hand they note everything else. They look at everything else, carefully, because that is what you do. The grief is entirely in the form — never said.
**Emotional register:** Meditative, vast, precise. The most distanced song on the record — which makes it, in its own way, the most exposed.
**Music:** cold play or muse feel think "speed of sound" or "starlight", Sparse and slow. Long spaces. The sound of something very far away.

---

### 8. *Orpheus (Facing Forward)*
**Form:** Myth retelling — direct, lyric, not quite a ballad
**Voice:** Orpheus after he looks back. After Eurydice is gone the second time.
**What it does:** Not the looking back — that story has been told. This is what happens after. What Orpheus does with his hands when they are empty. What the walk back up from the underworld looks like when there is nothing to walk toward. The myth asks: what is the shape of a life after the second loss?
**Emotional register:** Raw, rap british male voice. The grief of the person who tried everything and it still wasn't enough. No self-pity — just the plain fact of the empty hands.
**Music:** UK british rap indie influences, think rizzle kicks more upbeat cadance, acoustic plucked beat maybe some irish feel to it, lyrics more british style slang.

### 9. *Forgetting*
**Form:** Song — verse/chorus, language occasionally breaks mid-line
**Voice:** Interior — the bereaved person alone
**What it does:** The terror of forgetting. The forgetting that happens anyway despite the terror. The voice of her, growing less exact. The specific details — the way she said a particular word, the weight of her hand — blurring at the edges (especially when looking at other children or other people). And inside the forgetting: the love is still there, intact, even as the details soften. The song holds both the terror and the grace of it.
**Emotional register:** Afraid, then tender, then quietly broken. The language itself may start to blur toward the end — incomplete lines, words that don't arrive.
**Music:** British 2007 indie think hot hot heat, up-beat but instruments silver flute and lute

### 10. *The Second Moon*
**Form:** Speculative / lyric — short, precise, image-driven
**Voice:** Someone who can see it; addressing someone else who might
**What it does:** A second moon appeared some years ago, visible only to some people. Not everyone can see it. Those who can recognise each other by the way they look up. It does not make the night brighter. It makes it more specific. The song is about the community of the bereaved — the recognition, the not-being-alone, the particular vision that grief gives and takes simultaneously.
**Emotional register:** Quiet wonder, bittersweet. One of the warmer songs on the record without being consoling.
**Music:** british indie - cold play or muse feel think "speed of sound" or "starlight" but little more upbeat and almost lullaby like inthe lyrical structure. Long spaces. The sound of something very far away.

## The Core Idea

The word *okay* is the most violent word in the social vocabulary of grief.

"How are you?" is a question that assumes a reference point — a baseline of okay, a before, a normal that the person answering can be measured against. Childloss removes that reference point completely. There is no before to return to, no after to aim for. The person asking "how are you?" is, without knowing it, asking you to perform a version of yourself that no longer exists, in a language you no longer speak, in a world you no longer recognise as yours.

And yet — the word itself contains the exit. *Okay* doesn't have to mean *fine*. It doesn't have to mean *better*. It can mean: I am here. I am still breathing. The not-okay is its own country and I have learned to live in it, and that is, in the most radical and honest sense of the word — okay.

This album lives in that paradox. It does not resolve it. It walks around inside it, room by room.

## What This Album Is Not

- It is not a grief-recovery narrative. There is no arc from broken to healed.
- It is not a tribute or an elegy in the conventional sense — it is not *about* the lost child so much as it is *from* the person who remains.
- It is not a self-help record. It offers no comfort that it hasn't earned.
- It is not angry in a cathartic, release-valve way. The anger here is quieter and more permanent.

---

## What This Album Is

A record of the altered state. A document of the world that exists on the other side of the question "how are you?" — where the only honest answer takes too long to give, costs too much to give, and would empty the room if you gave it.

Songs from various vantage points, various time-distances, various emotional registers — some numb, some tender, some darkly absurd, some briefly transcendent, some devotional. No single song is the whole truth. The whole truth is the album.

## Philosophical Threads

### Hiraeth
The Welsh word for a grief-longing that has no direct English equivalent — a homesickness for a place or person you cannot return to, for a time that cannot be recovered. Unlike nostalgia it contains no sweetness. It is the ache of the irreversible. The album lives in hiraeth the way a house lives in weather.

### Mono no Aware
The Japanese aesthetic of *the pathos of things* — the deep feeling stirred by the transience of beautiful things. Traditionally this is the cherry blossom: beautiful because it falls. But what happens when the transient thing is a child? The philosophy doesn't prepare you for that. The album lives in the gap where the philosophy runs out.

### Kintsugi — and its limits
The Japanese art of repairing broken pottery with gold — the idea that the breaking is part of the object's history and the repair is part of its beauty. A useful metaphor, but a dangerous one: it implies the broken thing becomes more beautiful for having broken. The album will hold this idea carefully. Some songs may earn it. Others may refuse it. The refusal is just as honest.

### The Absurd (Camus)
The world does not pause. The supermarket keeps its doors open. The season turns without consultation. Tuesday arrives whether you are ready for it or not. Camus's absurd is the condition of a human being who demands meaning from a universe that offers none — and who revolts against that silence not by finding meaning but by continuing anyway. There is a version of "okay" that is exactly this revolt: not hope, not acceptance, just the refusal to stop.

### Kaddish
The Jewish prayer of mourning that does not mention death — that praises life, the world, God — spoken by the bereaved in public, in community, in the presence of others who witness. The theological content is not the point. The act is the point: the necessity of saying something out loud, in company, when what has happened is unspeakable. Several songs in this album are that act — not prayers exactly, but public speakings of what normally goes unsaid.

### The Thin Place
Celtic (particularly Irish and Welsh) spiritual concept: a *thin place* is a location where the boundary between this world and the next is unusually permeable — where the distance between the living and the dead is not a wall but a membrane. Not a supernatural claim; more a quality of attention. Certain moments, certain domestic acts, certain ordinary Tuesdays are thin places. The album will visit them.

### Love with Nowhere to Go
Not a formal philosophical tradition but perhaps the most honest description of grief available: grief *is* love, continued past the point where it has anywhere to land. It does not stop. It finds no object. It keeps generating. The question the album asks is not "how do you stop grieving?" but "what do you do with love that has nowhere to go?"

## Spiritual Threads

- **Devotion without doctrine.** Several songs should carry the weight and tone of prayer — not addressed to a god necessarily, but addressed, in the way that grief is always addressed: to someone, to something, to the absence that has a specific shape.
- **The name.** In many spiritual traditions, the name of the dead is sacred and must be spoken. There is a song in this album about the necessity — the urgency — of saying the name out loud, because the name is the last thing that proves the person existed.
- **Bearing witness.** Not fixing. Not healing. The act of simply standing next to someone in their not-okay and saying: I see this. Devotional in the most stripped-back sense.
- **Sacred ordinary.** The cup of tea. The empty chair. The coat still on the hook. The theological instinct of many traditions — that the sacred is not elsewhere, it is here, in the material world, in the specific object — is the instinct this album follows: the ordinary as reliquary.

## Vantage Points and Rooms of the Album

The album moves through distinct emotional and social spaces. Not every song needs to be from the same person or the same moment in time.

### The Social Mask
The performance of okay. The "I'm fine." The face you put on for the post office, the colleague, the birthday party. The exhaustion of that performance. The strange double consciousness of it — carrying the real thing inside while the performance plays out.

### Inside the Question
What it costs to be asked "how are you?" when you cannot answer it. Who is the question actually for? What is the asker really asking? The violence of the casual.

### The Ordinary Tuesday
Nothing happens. The world is just Tuesday. The cruelty of that. The way grief doesn't perform itself dramatically every day — sometimes it just sits there, in the Tuesday, in the light on the kitchen floor.

### The Angry Wednesday
Not dramatic operatic grief-anger. The quieter kind: the low-grade fury at the world for continuing, at people for not understanding, at yourself for still being here needing things like coffee and sleep.

### The Godchild / Other People's Children
The radical double-edge. You love the child in front of you. The love is real and full and present. And it does not cancel the other thing — the other thing is right here too, in the same moment, and both are true simultaneously and there is no word for that.

### The First Laugh
The moment you laugh at something — a joke, a film, an absurdity — and the guilt arrives with it. The feeling that laughter is a betrayal. And then, somewhere much further along the timeline: the understanding that laughter is not a betrayal. That it was never a betrayal.

### Spring
The insistence of it. Every year, without asking permission, the world renews. Flowers bloom in the garden of a house where a child is not. The beauty is unbearable and also, in some way, necessary.

### The Body
What grief does to the physical self. Sleep and the complicated negotiations around it. Appetite. The strange feeling of being inside a body that keeps running on its own without you.

### The Name
The necessity of saying it. The fear that other people will stop saying it. The way a name, said aloud enough, stays in the air a moment after.

### The Long Game
Not the raw early grief but the distant version. Three years in. Five years. The grief that is not acute but structural — built into the architecture of every day now, indistinguishable from the self. The version of okay that is tired, and quiet, and somehow still standing.

## Musical Palette

Not fixed — the album should allow different sonic worlds per song to reflect different emotional states. But some constants:

- Close-mic'd vocal, intimate — the listener is always in the same room
- Space and silence used structurally — what is *not* played matters
- No triumphant key changes, no conventional uplifting resolutions
- Field recordings for grounding: a garden, a kitchen, a street, rain on a window
- Where there is beauty in the sound, it should be the beauty of the particular, not the beautiful-in-general
- Some songs may be very simple — one instrument, one voice. The simplicity is not poverty; it is weight.

---

## Tone

This album is not a dark record. It is an honest one. Honesty sometimes means darkness; it also means an unexpected Tuesday joke, a real laugh, a crocus in February, a cup of tea that is exactly right. The record does not perform suffering. It witnesses it — from inside, from very close, in specific detail.

The word "okay" by the last track should mean something it didn't mean on the first track. Not better. Not resolved. Just — inhabited. Worn in. The country of not-okay, fully mapped.

---

## On the Name

The lost child is not named in this overview, but will be named in songs where it earns the naming. The name is not a detail. The name is the whole thing.
