# Orpheus (Facing Forward)
**Album:** Okay
**Track:** 8
**Created:** 2026-03-18

---

## The Myth — What It Carries

Orpheus descends into the underworld to bring Eurydice back. He earns the right. He plays the music and everything stops — even the stones weep, even Hades listens. The one rule: walk forward. Don't look back. She'll follow.

He looks back.

She's gone.

The version you know ends there. This one begins.

**What the myth carries:**

- Orpheus = the bereaved parent who tried everything, did everything right, got so close
- Eurydice = what was lost, twice: once to death, once to the looking back
- The underworld = grief itself, the long interior journey
- The walk back up = continuing to live with nothing at the end of the road
- His hands = the central image of the song; they were made to hold something; they are empty; what do you do with hands like that

The song asks not *why did he look back* — that story's done, the story's been told a thousand times — but: **what does Orpheus do on Tuesday morning?** What does he do with the lute? What does he do with the hands?

No self-pity. No wailing. Just the plain fact of what's left.

## Style
- Rizzle Kicks — upbeat, bouncy, UK rap/indie cadence; punchy delivery over a plucked acoustic groove with an Irish live-music energy
- UK British male, close and direct — not performing suffering, just reporting back from it; the upbeat cadence is the point, it's what makes the plainness land harder
- British/London slang woven in naturally — not as decoration, as the actual language the voice thinks in
- Sentence-to-sentence rhyme throughout, fast couplet pairs in verse, wider in the chorus
- The myth stays present — Orpheus is named, the myth is stated — but the emotional content is entirely contemporary and direct

## Instrumentation
- **Acoustic guitar:** energetically finger-picked, Irish folk feel — a driving, rhythmic strum with a melody plucked over the top; the spine of the whole track
- **Bodhrán (or programmed equivalent):** the pulse; steady, slightly insistent, gives it the Irish session energy
- **Bass:** warm, acoustic-ish tone; provides forward momentum without dominating
- **Tin whistle motif:** brief instrumental phrase after each chorus — three or four bars, unaccompanied, bright then slightly trailing — the music he used to play, the only thing that's left
- **Piano:** enters at bridge only — a few simple low chords, more weight, drops back out
- **No big drum fill, no key change, no uplift:** the bodhrán holds its pulse and the song ends at the same tempo it started

## Production Notes
- Tone is direct, plain British male rap/spoken word — quick and rhythmic in verses, slower and sung more openly in chorus
- The tin whistle motif is the emotional hinge: it's his music, it's still there, his hands can still do that, and it's nowhere near enough
- Bridge should feel like the one moment the mask of the upbeat cadence drops slightly — not collapse, just: plain speaking, slower, piano underneath
- Final chorus: bodhrán drops; acoustic guitar finger-picks alone underneath the vocal; tin whistle plays its phrase one last time and doesn't finish
- The song ends on the beat — does not fade
