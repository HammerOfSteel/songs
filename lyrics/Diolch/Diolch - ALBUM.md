# Diolch - ALBUM

**Labels:** Diolch, Album
**Created:** 2025-11-27
**Edited:** 2025-12-13

---

ALBUM CONCEPT

Everything I Am Grateful For,
The Things Too High I Am Not,
All I Wish I Could Say

Themes:
Bereavement • Child-loss • Warmth • Hope • Youth • Welsh cultural memory • Raw intimacy • Poetic vulnerability • Philosophical yearning • Spiritual undertones • Devotional murmurs • Family and foes (the familiar made sacred)

General Sound:
A blend of UK indie, alt-folk, soft post-rock, minimal Welsh hymnody, lo-fi acoustic textures, whispered electronics, and string-laden chamber ambience.
Think: Sufjan Stevens – Carrie & Lowell, Ane Brun, Sigur Rós’s softer edges, Novo Amor, Gwenno (in Welsh), and touches of devotional folk chanting.

A sonic palette of:
• Wind-rustled field recordings
• Piano softened like worn wool
• Sparse harp and nylon guitar
• Low hums like chapel organs
• Intimate breathing, creaking wooden floors
• Distant children’s laughter as memory ghosts

TRACKS

1. “Croeso” (You Are Welcome)
Verses in english,
Chorus in English but we use croeso as a welsh word.

Tone: Warm, inviting, devotional, like opening the door to a small chapel of memory.
Music Style: UK indie, folk-ambient, reel like, picked violin, tin flute

Description:
A welcome, come in an take part. We are here together, lay what you carry down and let us see,.


**2. “Rhaid Cropian Cyn Cerdded”

(You Must Crawl Before Walking)**
Tone: Tender frustration, slow becoming, early growth.
Music Style: UK indie, harp beats and steel drums, pan flutes.

Description:
A song about the ache of progress. About starting over after loss, learning to move again when the body feels made of grief. Welsh proverbs give it a folkloric grounding — a reminder that even healing must crawl first. Brushing and breaking again, getying up every single day

3. “SHWMAE!”

(Sut mae — How are things?)
Tone: Bright, youthful, playful; a sudden flare of daylight in the album.
Music Style: Indie-folk pop burst • swift tempo • handclaps • small choir harmonies.

Description:
A greeting turned into a song of reconnecting. The narrator tries to sound cheerful, but the subtext hints at longing. Joy and ache overlap — what Welsh does beautifully.

**4. “Rwy’n Barod i Roi’r Ffidil yn y Tô”

(I’m ready to put the fiddle in the roof — I give up)**
Tone: Raw, dark-folk, confessional.
Music Style: Sparse guitar • bowed bass • creaking textures • a slow emotional collapse.

Description:
The breaking point track. Exhaustion. A moment where weight becomes unbearable like the gravity of it all clopases into itself, folding onto itself. narrative like meting the devil at the cross roads playing the fiddle, ready to give up.

5. “Ta” (Thanks)

Tone: Small, humble gratitude.
Music Style: Soft irish guitar finger-picked, ambient pads, UK british rap/indie.
Lyrics: feel like a spell almost of rhyming words and sentence to sentence rhyme structure. Woven in an UK style british but everyday languange and full of sweetness and banterlike between father and daugther

Description:
A miniature interlude. sincere. A thank-you addressed to all the beauty that still is here, to all the gods of small things, the time I got with you - little mornings waking you up, little kisses on my head being woken up by you, little messy kitches disasters, daycare pickups, helloes - goodbyes, valley and seaside adventures, days of just us.

**6. “Oriau Man y Bore”

(The Small Hours of the Morning)**
Tone: Dreamlike, lonely-but-holy.
Music Style: Nocturnal ambient folk • piano drenched in reverb • distant field recordings.

Description:
A portrait of those fragile early hours when the world is silent and memories become loud. The narrator wanders through thought and prayer, hearing echoes of a child’s laughter or imagining warm breath on their shoulder.

**7. “Iawn, Diolch”

(Fine, thanks — the casual reply)**
Tone: Polite mask over aching truth.
Music Style: UK british rap/indie, acounstic plucked harp beat, steeldrums, undertones of irish feel, youthful lyrical style almost devotional in the banter

Description:
This song explores the emotional lie of “I’m fine.”
A commentary on performing normality while grieving.
The Welsh phrase adds cultural nuance — the everyday small talk hiding a storm.


**8. “Dim yn yr Un Cae”

(Not in the Same Field — Not in the Same League)**
Tone: Philosophical, self-reflective, comparing wounds and strengths.
Music Style: Acoustic Folk-rock swell • rolling percussion • choral Welsh harmonies.

Description:
A meditation on comparison: pain, talent, healing. Realising we cannot measure ourselves against others — each grief, each family, each history grows in its own field.
A powerful mid-album peak.

**11. “Diolch am Eich Amser”

(Thank You for Your Time)**

Tone: Closing prayer, gratitude, farewell, gentle acceptance.
Music Style: low-fi mixed with acoustic mandolin picked strings, UK british indie/rap • whispered last line.
Lyrics: sentence to sentence rhyming structure and flow, devotional undertones, warm and held, soft banter, metaphorical spiritual philosofical undertones mixed with a modern style of writing,

Description:
The narrator thanks the listener, the lost loved one, and the ancestors. The album ends not with an answer, but with presence. It's a devotional bow, a release. A Welsh goodbye full of warmth and ache.
