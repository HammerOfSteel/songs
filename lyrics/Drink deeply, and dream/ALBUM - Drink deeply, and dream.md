# ALBUM - Drink deeply, and dream

**Labels:** Dancing salamanders, Drink deeply, and dream
**Created:** 2024-09-07
**Edited:** 2024-09-09

---

An album inspired by those early conversations, themes of mental health ringing clear; depression, self loathing, us again the world, communion together in loneliness, loss and bereavement.
But also a strong message of hope breaking through, of self reliance, of humanity.


1. "A real smile"
Theme: The endless search for meaning in a world that feels vast and indifferent.
Narrative: A young girl hears the legend of a crow that visits the Silent Tor, a lonely hill where the winds never rest. Each visit, the crow scratches at the stones, slowly wearing them down as ages pass. The story weaves through Cornish folklore, where the Tor symbolizes the weight of sorrow and the crow represents the patient endurance of time. As the girl grows, she begins to understand that her own journey is much like the crow’s—persistent, unyielding, and quietly transformative.

2. "Beneath the Rowan’s Shade"
Theme: The pivotal moment where one chooses the path toward healing or despair.
Narrative: Deep in the Swedish woods, a traveler encounters an ancient rowan tree, believed to be a guardian against evil. The tree stands at a crossroads, and beneath its shade, the traveler must choose between two paths: one leading deeper into the forest’s darkness, the other toward the distant light of a village. The rowan, rich with folklore as a protector, offers no guidance, only shelter from the storm. The traveler knows that whichever path they choose will set the course for their soul’s journey.

3. "Whispers of the Selkie’s Shore"
Theme: The search for connection and identity in a world that feels isolating and strange.
Narrative: A person stands by the shores of Cornwall, where the legend of the Selkie—creatures who can shed their seal skins to become human—lingers in the mist. The sea whispers of lost love and forgotten identities as the narrator searches for a sense of belonging. The Selkie’s dual existence becomes a metaphor for the narrator’s own struggle to reconcile their human desires with the pull of something deeper and more ancient. The song weaves Cornish folklore into a narrative of searching for a true self in a world that often feels alien.

4. "The Weeping Yggdrasil"
Theme: Grieving the loss of loved ones and finding a way to continue.
Narrative: In the heart of a mystical forest, Yggdrasil, the great World Tree from Norse mythology, weeps for the souls lost to the passage of time. The narrator, mourning their own losses, sits beneath its branches, seeking solace in its ancient roots. The Yggdrasil, a symbol of life’s interconnectedness, offers no easy answers, only the knowledge that life and death are entwined. The song blends Scandinavian folklore with a meditation on the cyclical nature of grief and the quiet strength found in continuing onward.

5. "Daughters of the Fossegrim"
Theme: The allure of despair and the struggle to resist its pull.
Narrative: In the deep forests of Sweden, near a hidden waterfall, the Fossegrim—musical spirits known for their melancholy tunes—sing their mournful songs. The narrator hears their call, a haunting melody that promises release from pain. But as they draw near, they see the faces of lost souls who followed the Fossegrim into the water and never returned. The song is a dance with despair, where the narrator must decide whether to succumb to the sweet sadness or fight to keep their head above water.

6. "Lanterns of Lyonesse"
Theme: The glimmer of hope in the darkest times.
Narrative: Off the coast of Cornwall lies the sunken kingdom of Lyonesse, where lanterns are said to still burn beneath the waves. The narrator, lost in their own darkness, dreams of these lanterns, believing they can guide them home. The song tells of a night journey across the stormy sea, where the narrator follows the faint lights of Lyonesse, clinging to the hope that they will find land before the dawn. The legend of Lyonesse becomes a metaphor for the fragile yet enduring light of hope in the face of overwhelming despair.

7. "The Wren’s Secret Song"
Theme: The quiet resilience found in the small, often overlooked moments of life.
Narrative: In the winter woods of Cornwall, the wren—a bird sacred in many European traditions—sings a song that few can hear. The narrator, weary and broken, stumbles upon the tiny bird and listens. The wren’s song, though quiet and unassuming, carries the wisdom of survival through the harshest of winters. The song becomes a meditation on the small but vital acts of resilience that sustain us through our darkest days.

8. "The Serpent’s Knot"
Theme: The entanglement of self-loathing and the struggle to break free.
Narrative: Deep within an ancient forest, the narrator encounters a serpent coiled around a tree, its body forming a knot that seems impossible to untangle. The serpent, a symbol of both wisdom and danger, speaks in riddles, reflecting the narrator’s own thoughts of self-loathing. As the narrator struggles to unravel the knot, they realize that the serpent’s grip is as much their own as it is the creature’s. The song weaves together themes of self-reflection, entrapment, and the slow, painful process of breaking free from destructive thoughts.

9. "The Dream"
Theme: The pursuit of transformation and the hope that drives it.
Narrative: The narrator, an alchemist in search of the Philosopher’s Stone, dreams of a world where lead turns to gold and sorrow to joy. In this dreamscape, they wander through surreal landscapes filled with cryptic symbols and half-whispered truths. The Alchemist’s journey becomes a metaphor for the human struggle to transform pain into wisdom, darkness into light. The song ends with the realization that the true Stone is not a physical object but the inner transformation that comes from enduring life’s trials.

10. "The Moorland Maiden"
Theme: The enduring power of love and memory.
Narrative: On the lonely moors of Cornwall, the narrator remembers a maiden who once walked these lands, her presence now felt only in the wind and the wildflowers. The maiden, a figure from folklore who loved deeply but lost everything, becomes a symbol of the narrator’s own lost loves. The song is a bittersweet reflection on how love lingers long after those we love are gone, shaping the landscapes of our hearts as the moors shape the land.
