# Shapes I've Been

**Labels:** Album, chalice and cauldron
**Created:** 2025-04-05
**Edited:** 2025-04-13

---

Though not among the earliest Mabinogion texts, the tale of Taliesin holds deep mythic weight. It tells of Gwion Bach, a boy who accidentally drinks from Ceridwen’s cauldron of inspiration. Transformed and hunted, he shifts through many forms—hare, fish, bird, grain—until reborn as Taliesin, Wales’ most legendary bard.

Born of fire and silence, this is the story of a soul shifting through foster homes, identities, pain, and poetry—of a child who was told they were broken and became a storm instead. A fierce hymn for every voice that had to shapeshift to survive.

I have been in a multitude of shapes,
Before I assumed a consistent form.
I have been a sword, narrow, variegated,
I will believe when it is apparent.
I have been a tear in the air,
I have been the dullest of stars.
I have been a word among letters,
I have been a book in the origin.
I have been the light of lanterns,
A year and a half.
I have been a continuing bridge,
Over three score river mouths.

She said I was born wrong.
Too loud, too soft, too much, too gone.
Mama had moons behind her eyes,
Each one a voice, a lullaby, a scream, a lie.
One day she'd braid my hair,
Next she'd forget I existed, just a blank stare.
“I’m making you strong,” she’d say,
“Pain is a poem, you’ll write it someday.”
But I didn’t wanna write, I wanted peace.
I wanted cartoons, a warm fleece.
Instead, I stirred the pot, just like she asked—
Boiling hours in rooms full of masks.
Then one night, the pot spat back—
Three drops of knowing, burned me black.
And suddenly the world cracked open like bone—
I saw too much and I was all alone.

I ran from the fire, I ran from the flame,
She chased me in shadows, whispering my name.
I changed shape just to stay unseen,
A ghost in a hoodie with a heart unclean.
"You're not mine," she’d cry, "you stole the light!"
But I didn’t choose the truth that night.
I ran from the mother, I ran from the pain—
But the knowledge stuck like a bloodstain.

I been a daughter, a son, a maybe, a blur,
A child of silence, a voice unsure.
Foster house five, couch number ten,
Wrote my name in chalk, erased again.
“I’m not your project,” I told a caseworker,
She blinked. “But you’re so articulate.” Like murder.
Nobody sees the knife in the ink,
Only the pretty rhyme, never the brink.
I changed shape like clothes from the bin,
Each persona stitched on paper-thin skin.
One day a boy, one day a thought,
One day a scream, another day not.
And all the while, she hunted me down—
Not my mother, but her ghost in a gown.
“Give it back,” she’d hiss, “it was meant for him.”
But I drank the storm, let it fill my limbs.

Am I thief or vessel?
Am I broken or blessed?
If knowledge is poison,
Why does it rise in my chest?

I ran from the flame, but I carried the spark,
Wrote in the margins where it’s quiet and dark.
I changed shape but I couldn’t escape,
All these selves that wore my face like tape.
“You’re not real,” she cried, “you’re what I lost.”
But every name I’ve been came at a cost.
I ran through the stories, through hurt, through sin—
Till I found the first shape I’d ever been.

In juvie they made us sit in a circle.
"Tell the truth," the counselor murmured, purple
Bruises on my ribs from the last brawl,
I said nothing—just stared at the wall.
But at night I wrote.
Not to be read.
Just to keep me from joining the dead.
One poem, then two, then fifty more,
Till the pain got legs and walked to the door.
She came back once—my mom. Or her ghost.
Brought me a scarf. It smelled like toast.
“Did you ever love me?” I finally asked.
She blinked. “I don’t know. That’s in the past.”
That’s when I knew the past was mine.
Not hers to hold. Not hers to define.
I’d been a hundred selves and each one true—
But none of them owed her the person I grew.

I stopped at the flame and I sang it raw,
With my hands all blistered and my voice in awe.
I changed shape and I shed my skin,
And I found the voice I’d hidden within.
“You’re mine,” she wept, “you’re all that’s left.”
I kissed her forehead, soft as theft.
“I’m not yours,” I said, “I’m what I became—
A storm-born poet with a borrowed name.”

I have been in a multitude of shapes.
A bruise. A breath. A broken plate.
A son. A maybe. A shadow at dawn.
A drop of ink when the world moved on.
I have been silence, carved in glass.
I have been a scream the wind let pass.
But now I write. And now I speak.
And I am the poem she tried to keep.

The number of saints in scores,
Valiant men, golden their party.
Before kings a career of praise,
Warriors, no one was before them in demanding.
In straits, in expanse, in every need,
May they be a city to our body and our soul!
