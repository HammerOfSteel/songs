# Okay — Brainstorm Tracks

Mark chosen tracks with an `X` in the checkbox. These become the ones we develop.

---

## The Question / The Performance

- [ ] **How Are You** — Someone rehearsing answers to the question, trying each one, discarding it. Ends mid-attempt. (spoken piece / album opener)
- [ ] **Fine** — The word "fine" repeated across a whole song, each repetition meaning something different and slightly more untrue than the last
- [ ] **Getting There** — The phrase people say when they don't want to say fine. What "there" is. Whether you're moving toward it or away.
- [ ] **You Know How It Is** — The social shorthand that assumes a shared reference point. The joke is: they don't know. Nobody knows.
- [ ] **The Face** — The performance of composure in public. What it costs. The drive home after.
- [ ] **The Right Answer** — A catalogue of the answers that would be honest, and why none of them can be said aloud. Darkly wry.
- [ ] **Someone Asked Today** — A plain spoken account of a single moment: the question, the pause, the choice, the answer given, the real answer swallowed.
- [ ] **The Ones Who Don't Ask** — About the people who stopped asking. Whether that's worse or better. The strange relief of it. The loneliness of it.

## The Ordinary World Continuing

- [ ] **Aisle Seven** — The supermarket: a small thing (a cereal box, a pair of small wellies, a song on the radio) breaks through. In public. The walk to the car.
- [ ] **Tuesday** — Nothing happens. The light on the kitchen floor. The world continues without consultation. The cruelty of an ordinary day.
- [ ] **The Bins** — Taking the bins out. The neighbour waves. Normal things still require doing. The absurdity of that.
- [ ] **The Post** — A piece of post arrives addressed to her. What you do with it. What you can't do with it.
- [ ] **The Season Turned** — Without asking permission, it is spring again. The garden renewed. The crocus in the garden of a house where a child is not.
- [ ] **The Radio** — A song comes on you can't listen to anymore. The hand reaches for the dial. The half-second before.
- [ ] **The Clock** — Time passing. The way an hour can take three weeks and a year can take an afternoon. The body's relationship with clock time after.
- [ ] **Still Raining** — The weather's indifference. It rains. It doesn't know. The particular cruelty of a beautiful day.
- [ ] **The Coat** — Her coat on the hook. The physical object that is still there. All the decisions around it. The one you can't make yet.
- [ ] **The List** — A shopping list with her things still on it. Or a to-do list. The uncrossed-off items.

## The Body / The Physical Self

- [ ] **Still Here** — The body keeps running on its own without you. Sleep, appetite, the physical survival you did not choose. Weary tenderness.
- [ ] **Sleep** — The complicated negotiations with sleep. The chair. The not-wanting to wake. The wanting to stay in the place between.
- [ ] **Hunger** — The strange reappearance of appetite. The guilt of it. The first time food tasted like something again.
- [ ] **The Hands** — What the hands remember. The weight they carried. What they reach for now without thinking.
- [ ] **The Chair** — Sitting down. The body needing rest. The chair you sat in. All the chairs.
- [ ] **Breathing** — The autonomic fact of continued breathing. That it doesn't ask permission. A meditation on the involuntary.

## Grief in Company

- [ ] **The Birthday** — At another child's birthday party. You love this child. The love is real and present. The other thing is also present. Both hands full.
- [ ] **Both Hands** — The godchild or close child in front of you. Two-column lyric: what is said aloud, what is felt beneath. Neither cancels the other.
- [ ] **The Table** — A family gathering. The chair that is empty. The people carefully not looking at it. The one who mentions it. The one who doesn't.
- [ ] **The One Who Stopped Calling** — From the friend's perspective. The guilt of not knowing what to say. The phone they didn't pick up. The apology that came too late.
- [ ] **The One Who Kept Calling** — The friend who stayed. What they did right. How odd it is that it can't be repaid. Love that asks nothing back.
- [ ] **The Fifty Minutes** — The therapist mid-session who has nothing left to offer. The silence they leave. The compassionate portrait of professional limits.
- [ ] **The Vicar / The Doctor / The Official** — The person whose professional role requires them to process loss and who carries it home. Their private collapse.
- [ ] **The Group** — A grief support group. The strange comfort of people who understand and the strange loneliness of realising each grief is untranslatable even to them.
- [ ] **What He Said** — Something someone said, meaning well, that landed like a stone. Repeated, examined, the damage mapped without cruelty.
- [ ] **The New Person** — Meeting someone who never knew. The moment of deciding whether to say. What happens either way.
- [ ] **The Colleague** — The colleague who found out. The impossible professionalism of the days after. The office as a place you have to pass through.

## Time and Its Distortions

- [ ] **Three Years** — What three years looks like from the inside. Not better, not the same, just — different. The geography changed.
- [ ] **The Anniversary** — The day. Every year, it comes back. What that day feels like at one year, at five years, at ten. The way it doesn't dull — it just changes shape.
- [ ] **Before** — A brief visit to the before. Not nostalgic — just accurate. What the world was when the reference point existed. The song ends when the before ends.
- [ ] **The Age She Would Have Been** — Each birthday she doesn't have. The growing child in the imagination. The age she would be now.
- [ ] **The Things She Would Have Hated** — Wry and tender. A list of things in the world she would have had opinions about. The specificity of the imagined future.
- [ ] **The Things She Would Have Loved** — Same form; different key. The things that exist in the world she would have claimed.
- [ ] **Still Counting** — The number of days. The compulsion to count. The day you stopped counting. The day you started again.

## The Internal Landscape

- [ ] **The Angry Wednesday** — Low-grade fury at the world for continuing. The quiet version of anger. Not explosive — just permanently lit.
- [ ] **The Weight** — The physical metaphor of carrying. What carrying means when the weight is grief. The impossibility of putting it down and the impossibility of not.
- [ ] **The Room** — The room that is still the same. The decision not to change it. The decision to change it. Either one is unbearable.
- [ ] **The Fog** — The early weeks. The way the anaesthetic of shock works. Fog as mercy and fog as terror.
- [ ] **The Stone** — Grief as a stone in the chest. Where it sits. Its exact weight and temperature. The scar tissue around it.
- [X] **The Good Day** — A day when it was almost okay. The guilt of that. The way a good day can be as hard as a bad one.
- [ ] **The First Time I Laughed** — The thing that made you laugh. The guilt. The much-later understanding that it was never a betrayal.
- [ ] **The Dream** — The dream where she is alive. The waking. The half-second of not-knowing. The waking again.
- [ ] **The Not Dreaming** — The nights she doesn't visit. The strange grief of that absence inside the absence.
- [X] **Forgetting** — The terror of forgetting. The forgetting that happens anyway despite the terror. The love inside the forgetting.

## The Language of Grief

- [ ] **There Are No Words** — The cliché that is also true. The attempt to say it anyway. The sentence that breaks in the middle.
- [ ] **The Untranslatable** — A Welsh proverb rendered in English. The word that has no English equivalent. Hiraeth as a song, not a concept.
- [ ] **The Name** — Not the final Tima song — an earlier, indirect approach. The importance of saying the name. The fear that others will stop saying it. The act of saying it out loud.
- [ ] **Elegy** — A formal elegy that knows it's a formal elegy and keeps interrupting itself because the form isn't big enough.
- [ ] **What We Don't Say** — The things that go unsaid in grief. The spaces between sentences. The silence that carries more than the words.

## The Devotional / Spiritual

- [ ] **I Don't Pray** — And yet. The address that happens anyway. The thing you say to the ceiling at 3am that is not a prayer and is not nothing.
- [X] **The Thin Places** — A moment when the membrane goes thin. Not supernatural — just true. The quality of attention in certain ordinary moments.
- [ ] **Bearing Witness** — A song about the act of simply standing next to someone. Not fixing. Not helping. Just: I see this. The devotional stripped bare.
- [ ] **The Candle** — Lighting a candle. The gesture that is and isn't religious. What the flame does. What it's for. What it can and can't do.
- [ ] **The Garden** — Not the Garden of Small Miracles — a different garden. The Biblical resonance of a garden as a place of loss and of witness. Gethsemane quietly in the background.
- [ ] **What Remains** — An inventory of what persists after. Love, clearly. But also: particular objects, the memory of a voice, a habit she had. A devotion to the specific.
- [ ] **Psalms for Tuesday** — One small psalm for an unremarkable day. The sacred in the quotidian. The form of a psalm without the theology.

## The Body of the Album / Meta-Songs

- [X] **Okay** — The final possible song. Not Tima — earlier. A direct address to the word. What it means now. The redefining. Can okay coexist with permanent not-okay? Yes. This is how.
- [ ] **The Reference Point** — The song about having no reference point. What "normal" used to mean. The impossibility of returning to a baseline that no longer exists.
- [ ] **The Country** — Grief as a country you live in now. Its geography, its weather, its customs. The fact that most people are just visiting and you are a resident.
- [ ] **Permission** — A song that gives permission. Not to be okay. Not to recover. Permission to be exactly where you are, for as long as it takes, which is forever, and that is okay.
- [ ] **The Album Explains Itself** — Wry, meta, self-aware. The song that says: here is why this record exists, here is what it cannot do, here is what it is trying to do instead.
- [ ] **Nobody Tells You** — The things about grief nobody tells you. Not the big things — the small ones. The weird ones. The ones that turn out to be universal but feel completely private.
- [ ] **The Exit Interview** — Dark and absurd. The idea of grief as a job you didn't apply for. The hypothetical exit interview. What you'd say.

## Tima — The Final Approach

- [ ] **Tima** — Builds from near-silence to full voice. Starts plain, each verse adds a layer. The name arrives at the peak. The music holds the note and fades without resolving. *(Album closer — the name spoken once, here only)*
- [ ] **What She Knew** — The things she understood that only she understood. The specific wisdom of a small person who saw everything clearly.
- [ ] **The Things She Said** — Verbatim or near-verbatim. The exact sentences. Held in the song like specimens under glass. The reverence of quotation.
- [ ] **The Hippo** — An object. A specific one. What it is now. What it was then. The weight of a soft toy.
- [ ] **Red Wellies** — Another object. The specificity of grief: not a child, but this child, identified by this thing, this colour, this size.
- [ ] **Toast** — Cut in triangles. The specific preference. The domestic love language. The toast still made that way even now.

## Through a Different Lens — Alternative Forms & Modes
*These approach the same subject through a completely different mode of writing — fable, allegory, absurdism, fantasy, myth, found documents, etc. The grief is present but not named as grief; the form carries it.*

---

### Fable / Folk Tale / Myth

- [ ] **The Bird Who Kept Returning** — A fable. A bird returns every morning to a nest that is empty. It does not understand empty. It brings what it has always brought. The nest does not fill. The bird returns the next day. No moral given.
- [ ] **The River That Ran Backwards** — A folk tale. A river in a valley decided one day to run back to its source. Everyone said this was impossible. The river was not interested in what was possible. Song about the refusal of natural law in grief.
- [ ] **The Fox Who Carried a Stone** — A fable. A fox finds a smooth white stone and carries it everywhere. Other animals ask why. The fox cannot explain. The stone does not get lighter. The fox does not put it down. Life continues around this fact.
- [ ] **The Kingdom Where Time Stopped** — A fairy tale. A kingdom where a clock stopped at a particular hour and the king ordered nothing be touched, no hour passed, no door opened. What grows in a kingdom that has sealed itself in one moment. Beauty that goes wrong.
- [ ] **The Selkie** — The old myth: a creature that took off its skin to be human and cannot find the skin again. The world it walked into was real and warm and worth it. The skin is gone. It cannot go back. It does not know how to live entirely in either world now.
- [ ] **The Star You're Looking At** — A myth told as plain fact. The light from a star takes a thousand years to reach us. You are looking at something that no longer exists. You do not stop looking. The light does not stop travelling. This is not a metaphor — it is a description.
- [X] **Orpheus (Facing Forward)** — A retelling. Not the looking back — that's been done. This is what happens after he looks back. After Eurydice is gone again. After the second loss. What Orpheus does with his hands when they are empty for the second time.
- [X] **Penelope** — She unweaves what she weaved. Every night she undoes the day. She is not waiting for Odysseus. She is waiting for the thing before Odysseus. She is waiting for a world that has already ended.
- [ ] **The Last Lighthouse** — A ballad. A lighthouse keeper who keeps the light burning for ships that no longer come. The shipping lanes changed years ago. He knows. He keeps the light burning anyway. Not hope — practice. The keeping as the point.
- [ ] **The Cartographer's Grief** — A myth. A cartographer whose maps were perfect. Then the territory changed — one feature removed, overnight, without notice. Every map is now wrong. She keeps making maps anyway because the making is what she does, and wrong maps are still maps.

---

### Absurdist / Dark Comedy / Bureaucratic

- [X] **Form 7B** — Absurdist. Grief tries to file a report. The office has no category for this. It is too large for the boxes on the form. Transferred to another department. Put on hold. The hold music plays. This is a song about the hold music.
- [ ] **The Exit Interview** — Already in the list but expanding the form: fully absurdist, played straight. HR has scheduled a meeting to discuss the loss. What are your learnings? What would you do differently? How would you rate the experience? Dark comedy that turns on itself.
- [ ] **Customer Service** — On hold for a very long time. The problem cannot be resolved remotely. No one is available to take your call. Your grief is important to us. Please hold. Song about being on hold. Song about the thing you're on hold about.
- [ ] **The Waiting Room** — Medical and existential. A person in a waiting room. The magazine is from last year. The chair is uncomfortable. Their name is never called. The waiting room has been their address for some time now. Absurdist but deeply sad.
- [X] **The Lost Property Office** — Bureaucratic surrealism. Someone tries to report something lost at the lost property office. They cannot describe it in the terms the form requires. Size? Shape? Where did you last have it? The form is for objects. This is not an object.
- [ ] **The Weather Report** — A weather forecast delivered straight. Grief reported in meteorological terms: low pressure system, no visibility, conditions expected to persist. The forecaster is professional. The forecast is permanent. An absurd, flat reading of a devastating climate.
- [ ] **The Nature Documentary** — Narrated in David Attenborough cadences, clinically, compassionately distanced. "Here we observe the grieving parent in its natural habitat. It moves slowly. It performs the rituals of the living. It is indistinguishable from the others, if you don't know what you're looking for."

---

### Allegory / Something That Seems Like Something Else

- [ ] **The Flooded House** — A song about a house that flooded. The water came in. Some rooms were salvageable. Others were not. The decisions about what to keep, what to clear, what to leave. Entirely literal on the surface. About nothing else on the surface. About everything underneath.
- [ ] **The Gardener** — A gardener tends a garden. Plants something. Waits. What comes up is not what was planted. Or nothing comes up. Or something beautiful comes up in the wrong place. The gardener does not stop gardening. No grief is named. It is entirely about grief.
- [ ] **The Cartographer's Second Map** — A person making a map of a place that exists only in memory. The edges keep moving. The roads have changed. The map is wrong by the time it is finished. They keep making it. The map is the love.
- [X] **The Astronomer** — A person who spends their life looking at things that are unreachably far away. One night the sky is clear and one thing is missing. They keep the log. They note the absence. They look at everything else, carefully, because that is what you do.
- [ ] **The Archivist** — Someone whose job is to preserve. To keep things exactly as they were. Temperature-controlled rooms. Acid-free paper. The measures taken to prevent decay. The thing that cannot be preserved regardless. The archivist who does it anyway.
- [ ] **The Ferryman** — Based on Charon, but human. A person whose life is carrying people across from one side to the other. What it costs to do that indefinitely. What they see in the faces of the people going. The shore they never reach themselves.
- [ ] **The Clockmaker** — A clockmaker fixes clocks. A particular clock stopped at a particular time. They know exactly how to fix it. They do not fix it. The other clocks tick. That one is still.
- [ ] **The Empty Boat** — A traditional image: a boat with no one in it, drifting. Where it came from. Where it is going. What it carried. What it means to find it. Ballad form. Ancient and plain.

---

### Children's Story (That Isn't For Children)

- [ ] **The Very Heavy Stone** — In the style of a picture book: a person carries a very heavy stone. They try to put it down. The stone comes with them. They learn to carry it differently. They learn it is part of them now. Slightly darker than it sounds. The form of the children's book doing emotional work that the direct form cannot.
- [ ] **The House With All the Lights On** — A story about a house where someone keeps all the lights on. Upstairs, downstairs, every room. The neighbours wonder. The person knows why. The lights are on. That is enough. Plain, quiet, completely devastating if you know.
- [ ] **The Giant Who Sat Down** — A giant who was always moving, always carrying, one day sat down and did not get up. Not because they couldn't. Because sitting was right for now. The world moved around the sitting giant. That was also right. A very short song. Maybe almost nothing.

---

### Fantasy / Speculative

- [ ] **The World With One Colour Missing** — A fantasy world in which one colour has been removed. Not dramatically — it was just gone one morning. People who never knew it can't see the gap. People who did see it can't describe it to those who didn't. Absolutely about one specific thing.
- [ ] **The Door That Opens the Wrong Way** — A magical-realist piece. A door in the house that opens onto somewhere else. Not the past — somewhere else. The person opens it sometimes. Never goes through. The door is always there.
- [ ] **The Library of What Didn't Happen** — A fantasy. A library that holds all the futures that didn't occur. Each one shelved by year and name. A person who goes there and finds the shelf. Cannot open the book. Comes back every year to the same shelf.
- [X] **The Second Moon** — A short speculative piece. A second moon appeared some years ago, visible only to some people. Not everyone can see it. Those who can recognise each other by the way they look up. The second moon does not make the night brighter. It makes it more specific.
- [ ] **The Map With a Blank Space** — Old cartographers wrote *here be dragons* on the places they hadn't charted. A song about the blank space. What does get written there. What it means to live at the edge of the known map.

---

### Poem-as-Song / Formal Structures

- [ ] **Inventory** — A pure list poem. An unadorned inventory of what is left. No sentences, no verbs. Just the items. The form making the argument.
- [ ] **Call and No Response** — A call-and-response structure where only one side speaks. The call goes out. No response. The call goes out again. No response. The call changes over time — shorter, quieter, less expecting. The form is the subject.
- [X] **Villanelle for a Tuesday** — Using the villanelle form (two lines that repeat and return) to trap the same Tuesday, the same ordinary day, in its repetition. The form as grief's structure — you keep returning to the same lines whether you want to or not.
- [ ] **The Ode to Nothing** — A formal ode addressed to absence. The ode form is for celebrating. This is an ode to nothing. The form strains against its own content. The tension between the celebratory form and the thing it's addressed to is the whole song.
