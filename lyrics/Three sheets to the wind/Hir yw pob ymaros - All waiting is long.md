Ah, but a rare bird is rare. It is when one is not looking, at times one is not there That it comes. You must wear your eyes out, as others their knees. I became the hermit of the rocks habited with the wind and the mist. There were days, so beautiful the emptiness it might have filled, its absence was as its presence; not to be told any more, so single my mind after its long fast, my watching from praying.

Toast gone cold, tea gone black, she’s at the window—won’t turn back. Lion in arms, sock half on, watching the lane where the car's long gone.

“He's coming home today, isn’t he?”

He nods—"Yes love, he's coming, you see me."

He pours the milk too fast, it spills, she giggles soft like time stands still. She taps the glass—draws moons and rain, say;

“I forgot how he smells again.”

Peter just smiles, tucks her in tight, says, “He’ll be back by tea tonight.”

All waiting is long,

when the sky don’t know your name. But we hold the light in buttered bread, in tea that never came. He's not here yet, but we still sing, in quiet cups, in lion wings.

Park bench hums in a soft white hush, thermos warm, but the thoughts still rush. Dog by my boots, snow in my beard, waiting for the bell like I’ve always feared. That moment too still, too clear, too wide, like memory knocking from the other side. And there she is jam on her chin, Peter’s coat, soft winter grin. I blink. The frost don’t melt, but something in me feels it felt. Waiting's a loop, a hush, a hum, where past and present slowly drum.

The old know, and the young suspect,
the old ones know, the young just feel ya.

All waiting is long,

but we still sit straight. Counting joy in second steps, in shoes that hesitate. She’s not here now, but still she stays, in the way the breeze just knows her ways.

London blurs, my breath’s gone thin, coach hums loud like it’s holding sin. Ticket in pocket, hand on chest, ten more bends till the place I rest.

Kitchen clatter, toast half bit, Peter’s laughing, “She can’t sit.” She spins on her chair, scarf too wide, asks, “Is he coming?” He just smiles inside. Back on the coach, I pass the sign, Newport stretch and I cross the line. Bus door hisses—she’s there in gold, arms like stars, eyes so bold. I lift, she squeals, and time lets go just one hug in the cold, clean snow.

All waiting is long,

but the run was worth the years. Her arms, her laugh, the salt in tears. No march, no hymn, just coat and sky and the kind of love you don’t ask why.

Backseat buzz, the heater low, her head on my chest like falling snow. Peter’s half asleep with his cap pulled down, she hums a song with a sleepy frown. “I missed you, Papa. Next time stay,”

I said, “Next time, love. I’ll find a way.” She points at the trees, counts to four, says, “They’re different now than before.” Peter snorts, says, “You growing wise?” She grins and shrugs—truth in her eyes. Co-op sign and the hill we know, Crosskeys waits in the bus light glow. And if this is life, then this is right a girl, her uncle, and a winter night.

The clock don’t chime, but the breath still keeps. We wait through years, and laugh in sleeps. Some waits hurt, but others shine. And some live on inside the line.

School doors open. There he is gold curls, wide grin, same look she had when she'd begin. I stand. Arms out. One more wait ends. And still, another begins.
