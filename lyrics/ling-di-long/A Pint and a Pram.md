# A Pint and a Pram

**Labels:** ling-di-long
**Created:** 2025-04-15
**Edited:** 2025-04-15

---

“I said we’d be here at one.”
“It’s one-twenty. That’s not bad.”

Tima’s already throwing chips.
Peter’s ordered shots.
Steph’s brought suncream and six napkins.
Mam says, 
"God help us,” 
but she’s smiling when she says it.

The table’s sticky. The pram’s wedged in like royalty.
Newport’s sky is half threat, half poetry.
And here we are —
All grown up.
Kind of.

Tima’s got apple juice with a straw she won’t use,
A pirate hat, no shoes, and a vendetta against napkins.
Peter’s got crisps he won’t share,
Steph’s brought oat milk for a baby who wants chips.

Mam’s got that half-glare, half-grin thing down,
Says, 
“I swear this table wobbles on purpose.”
I try to order food like I’ve still got agency —
“One pint, please. Two hands. No dignity.”

Tima throws a chip, and we cheer.
Eira’s late. We save her a seat with a teething ring.
Some lad in the corner says “You alright, mate?”
And I say, “Living the dream. Extra ketchup.”

A pint and a pram and no real plan,
Just love on tap and chips in hand.
We’re wild and wiped and three months behind,
But still here — still the best of our kind.

The sun makes everyone forgiving.
There’s cider spills and stories spilling.
Steph’s got Mam laughing with some half-true tale
About the time I tried to build a cot and nearly failed.

Tima’s asleep now — miracle or mistake?
Wrapped in her cardigan, dreams on her face.
Someone walks past and says, “Lovely age.”
I nod like I know what to do with this stage.

Peter’s humming a song no one knows.
I lean back, my back hurts, my heart glows.
We’re not young — not like we were —
But we’re louder, kinder, more unsure.

And here, in this plastic chair
With a half-drunk pint and teething despair,
I feel free.
Like I did when freedom was simpler
And didn’t wear bibs.

A pint and a pram in a garden square,
Crumbs in the air, juice everywhere.
It’s curry chips and a half-missed song,
And I’ve never felt this right or wrong.

We talk about gigs we didn’t go to.
Places we’ll take them, maybe one day soon.
Steph holds Tima while I breathe for a sec.
Peter offers crisps like communion and regret.

The sky goes orange and soft and strange,
Newport’s roofs catching fire then fading again.
Mam’s resting her head on her hand,
Watching Tima dream, watching the land.

I say, “You think they’ll remember this?”
Mam says, 
“Maybe. Maybe just the smell of the chips.”

I sip what’s left. It’s warm and flat.
But so am I. And that’s not bad.

This isn’t freedom like I once thought —
It’s better. It’s louder. It comes with snot.
It’s not escape, it’s not the past —
It’s a pint and a pram, and a laugh that lasts.

A pint and a pram and a table too small,
But we make it work — make room for it all.
We’re messy, late, and halfway grown —
But this — this chaos — this is home.

Tima wakes up, asks 
“Are we still at the party?”

I say,
“Yeah, cariad. And the party never ends —
just changes its bedtime.”
