Narrative for "The Work"
Amelia’s journey begins as she finds herself in a dreamlike initiation, introduced to the concept of transformation—not as a destination, but a grueling path of personal growth. Her guide, who appears more as a presence than a person, teaches her that the work is not external but internal, requiring perseverance, self-reflection, and surrender to failure.

In her dream, she explores forgotten places along Cornwall’s coast—ancient quarries, hidden coves, and lost trails—each symbolizing a piece of herself that must be uncovered and reconciled. The abyss, the mirror, and the ladder represent the stages of early transformation: recognition, descent, and effortful climbing. She doesn’t fully understand the process yet, only sensing that the journey has begun.

(Verse 1)
I found myself on an unnamed path,
The cliffs whispered riddles that the wind laughed past.
A guide appeared, though I never saw his face,
Only his voice, like tides pulling me into place.

He said, "The work begins not with answers sought,
But in the quiet places where you think you're lost."
At the edge of the world, by a cove long gone,
I took the first step as the night pressed on.

(Chorus)
It’s a climb with no summit, a mirror with no frame,
A question unanswered, always the same.
Through failures that beckon, through shadows that call,
The work isn’t rising—it’s learning to fall.

Not who I was, but what I could be.
The abyss stared back with its hollow gaze,
And I felt the work in the slowest of days.
